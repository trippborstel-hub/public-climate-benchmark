ESRS E1-1 · CDP C3
Transition Plan for Climate Change Mitigation
Comprehensive

Nike's climate transition strategy is anchored in its Move to Zero campaign — a long-term initiative targeting zero carbon and zero waste across the company's entire value chain. The plan is backed by SBTi-validated targets aligned with a 1.5°C pathway for Scope 1 & 2 emissions, with a well-below 2°C pathway for Scope 3. Nike uses a dual-baseline approach: FY2015 for SBTi long-term targets and FY2020 for interim 2025 operational targets.

The company's transition strategy prioritizes renewable energy procurement (96% renewable electricity achieved), sustainable materials innovation (Nike Forward, Flyknit, recycled polyester), supplier decarbonization (60%+ of production volume from suppliers with science-aligned targets), and circular design principles. Notably, Nike has not set a formal net-zero target year, distinguishing it from peers like Adidas and Puma who have committed to 2050 net zero.

Analyst Assessment

Nike is a leader in operational decarbonization among apparel/footwear companies, with 96% renewable electricity and a 69% reduction in Scope 1 & 2 emissions. The fundamental challenge is Scope 3: supply chain manufacturing and raw materials represent ~98% of Nike's total footprint. Achieving the 30% intensity reduction in Scope 3 by 2030 requires continued supplier engagement at scale — over 60% of production volume already comes from suppliers with science-aligned targets. The absence of a formal net-zero commitment is a notable gap versus peers.

Decarbonization Roadmap
FY2015 — SBTi Baseline
Nike establishes FY2015 as the baseline for SBTi long-term targets. Total Scope 1 emissions at 57K tCO₂e. The company begins scaling renewable energy procurement and sustainable materials innovation.
FY2019 — SBTi Validation
Science Based Targets initiative validates Nike's near-term targets: 65% reduction in S1+2 and 30% S3 intensity reduction by 2030. Nike joins RE100, committing to 100% renewable electricity. Total footprint at 11.0M tCO₂e.
FY2020 — Operational Baseline Set
FY2020 established as baseline for interim 2025 targets. Move to Zero campaign fully launched. Nike FY20 Impact Report published with comprehensive emissions inventory. COVID-19 disruptions temporarily reduce supply chain activity.
FY2023 — Accelerating Progress
Total footprint drops to 9.5M tCO₂e. 78% of Nike, Jordan, and Converse products contain recycled material. Nike Forward material platform launches with 75% lower carbon footprint than traditional fleece. 100% waste diversion at strategic supplier sites.
FY2024 — Current Position
Total footprint reaches 8.3M tCO₂e (↓25% vs. FY2019). 96% renewable electricity globally. 69% S1+2 reduction vs. FY2020. 36% S3 reduction in manufacturing & transport. Less than 1% inbound air freight. Revenue ~$51.4B — footprint declining while revenue grows.
FY2025 — Interim Target Year
Target: 70% S1+2 reduction (owned/operated) vs. FY2020. Maintain S3 at or below FY2020 levels. Achieve 100% renewable electricity. Most FY2025 targets are on track or already exceeded.
2030 — SBTi Target Year
−65% absolute Scope 1+2 vs. FY2015. −30% Scope 3 per unit of value added vs. FY2015. Continue scaling sustainable materials, supplier engagement, and circular design across all product categories.
Beyond 2030 — Move to Zero
Move to Zero campaign continues without a formally committed net-zero year. Focus on eliminating remaining operational emissions and driving Scope 3 reductions through material innovation and supply chain transformation. Net-zero commitment remains a gap versus sector peers.

Progress Assessment

Nike has substantially exceeded its interim FY2025 Scope 3 targets and is within 1 percentage point of its S1+2 target. The 2030 SBTi targets remain the key benchmark. The absence of a net-zero commitment is increasingly conspicuous as peers (Adidas: 2050 net zero, Puma: climate neutrality by 2050, H&M: net positive by 2040) have set long-term commitments. Nike's use of an intensity metric for Scope 3 (per unit of value added) also draws scrutiny — absolute Scope 3 targets are considered more ambitious.

Nike's energy strategy is built around three pillars: renewable electricity procurement (PPAs, virtual PPAs, and direct contracts), energy efficiency improvements at headquarters, distribution centers, and retail locations, and logistics optimization to minimize transportation emissions. The 96% renewable electricity achievement — up from much lower levels just five years ago — has been the primary driver of operational decarbonization.

Nike's distribution centers are LEED-certified, and the company has reduced inbound air freight to less than 1% of total goods movement — a significant logistics decarbonization lever. Nike's owned operations represent only ~2% of total emissions, so the energy transition impact on total footprint is limited. The real energy challenge is in Nike's contracted manufacturing supply chain, where supplier energy procurement is critical.

Climate Risk Identification & Resilience
Partial

Nike acknowledges climate-related risks in its SEC filings (10-K) and sustainability reporting, referencing physical, regulatory, and market risks across its global supply chain. Nike's asset-light model — with over 500 contracted manufacturing facilities, primarily in Vietnam, China, and Indonesia — concentrates physical risk exposure in regions highly vulnerable to climate impacts. However, detailed scenario analysis assumptions and quantified financial risk assessments are not fully disclosed in public reports.

Gap Assessment

Nike's climate risk disclosures are directional rather than quantified. The company's 10-K acknowledges climate-related risks but does not provide detailed TCFD-aligned scenario analysis results, quantified financial impacts, or asset-level exposure assessments in public reports. Given Nike's heavy concentration of manufacturing in climate-vulnerable SE Asian countries (Vietnam alone accounts for ~50% of footwear production), more granular physical risk quantification would significantly strengthen disclosures. Under ESRS E1-2 and E1-3, Nike would need to provide explicit scenario assumptions, time-bounded financial impact estimates, and resilience assessments.

ESRS E1-7 · CDP C11
Carbon Credits, Offsets & Removals
Partial

Nike's climate strategy is firmly reductions-first — the company's SBTi-validated targets are gross reduction targets with no reliance on carbon credits or offsets to meet stated goals. Nike does not make carbon-neutral product claims and has not disclosed any significant carbon offset purchasing programs. This positions Nike in line with best practice under SBTi and ESRS frameworks, which require that credits not be counted toward emission reduction targets.

However, Nike's public disclosures provide limited detail on any use of carbon credits for voluntary offsetting, internal carbon pricing mechanisms, or investments in carbon removal technologies. The company's approach appears to prioritize tangible operational changes — renewable energy, sustainable materials, supplier engagement — over market-based carbon instruments.

Analyst Note

Nike's reductions-first approach is commendable and aligned with best practice. However, the lack of any disclosed carbon removal strategy or investment — even as a complement to reduction efforts — may become a gap as peers begin investing in removal technologies. With ~8M tCO₂e of annual Scope 3 emissions, Nike will likely need some form of removal or neutralization strategy if it eventually sets a net-zero target. The complete absence of an internal carbon price is also notable for a company of Nike's scale.

ESRS E1-9 · E1-11 · CDP C3.4
Anticipated Financial Effects
Partial

As a publicly traded company, Nike provides detailed financial reporting through SEC filings. However, the company's climate-specific financial disclosures remain limited — Nike does not separately quantify climate-related CapEx, OpEx, or the financial impacts of physical and transition risks in a manner aligned with ESRS E1-9 requirements. Climate investments are embedded within broader operational and innovation budgets.

CSRD Readiness Gap

ESRS E1-9 and E1-11 require quantified financial effects from physical and transition risks, reconciled to financial statements. Nike currently does not provide climate-specific CapEx/OpEx breakdowns, asset-level risk exposure, or financial impact estimates in public disclosures. While Nike's operational emissions are relatively small (~3% of total), the financial exposure from supply chain disruptions in climate-vulnerable manufacturing regions (Vietnam, Indonesia, China) could be material. As a U.S.-listed company, Nike is not directly subject to CSRD but may face increasing pressure from European operations and investor expectations.

Governance & Accountability
Comprehensive

Nike has established robust climate governance with board-level oversight through the Corporate Responsibility, Sustainability & Governance Committee, comprised of independent directors. The company's sustainability efforts are led by Chief Sustainability Officer Noel Kinder, a 25+ year Nike veteran who previously served as VP of Sustainable Manufacturing & Sourcing and GM of Nike Vietnam. Climate and sustainability metrics are integrated into Nike's strategic planning and reporting frameworks.
