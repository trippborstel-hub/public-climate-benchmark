ESRS E1-1 · CDP C3
Transition Plan for Climate Change Mitigation
Comprehensive

Danone published its Climate Transition Plan in December 2023 — one of the most detailed in the food and beverage sector. As the first company globally to receive approved SBTi FLAG (Forest, Land and Agriculture) targets, Danone has set an ambitious precedent for agricultural emissions reduction. The company has operated as a Société à Mission (purpose-driven company under French law) since 2020, embedding sustainability into its corporate charter.

The transition plan covers all scopes and is structured around seven decarbonization levers spanning sustainable sourcing, dairy transformation, product development, packaging, logistics, subcontractor engagement, and direct energy use across 58,000+ farmer partners in 20 countries.

Analyst Assessment

Danone is the gold standard for climate governance in food & beverage. Triple A CDP score for 5 consecutive years, first SBTi FLAG approval, and Société à Mission status set it apart from peers. The methane action plan targeting 30% reduction from dairy is sector-leading and addresses one of the most difficult emission sources in the food system. The key risk is execution speed across 58,000+ farmer partners in 20 countries — the scope of supply chain transformation required is enormous.

Danone's energy strategy is anchored in the Re-Fuel Danone program, targeting 100% renewable electricity by 2030 and 50% total renewable energy across all operations. The company is pursuing a diversified mix of renewable sources including biogas from dairy waste, biomass, solar PV, green hydrogen pilots, and crop waste-to-energy conversion — reflecting its unique position as a dairy-heavy food company.

Danone's approach to thermal energy is particularly innovative, leveraging biogas generated from its own dairy supply chain and investing in biomass infrastructure at manufacturing plants across Europe and Asia.

Danone has conducted comprehensive climate scenario analysis using three IPCC scenarios, examining both physical and transition risks across its dairy, waters, and specialized nutrition businesses. As a company with significant dairy exposure and global water operations, Danone faces acute climate risks that are central to its core business model.

ESRS E1-7 · CDP C11
Carbon Credits, Offsets & Removals
Partial

Danone takes a reduction-first approach to carbon management, prioritizing direct emissions reductions over offsets. The company co-founded the Livelihoods Carbon Fund in 2011 — one of the earliest corporate-backed carbon fund initiatives — and has since expanded to a second fund. Danone's strategy increasingly focuses on insetting via regenerative agriculture within its own supply chain rather than purchasing third-party credits.

Danone's evian brand became the first carbon-neutral certified water brand in 2020, combining reduction efforts with high-quality offset projects. The broader corporate strategy, however, does not rely on credits to meet its SBTi targets.

ESRS E1-9 · E1-11
Anticipated Financial Effects
Partial

As a publicly traded company, Danone provides more financial transparency than many peers. However, granular financial effects from climate risks are not broken out separately in line with full ESRS E1-9 and E1-11 requirements. Climate investment is embedded within business group plans rather than disclosed as a standalone figure.

Disclosure Gap

While Danone provides a high-level climate risk materiality figure (47% of trading operating profit), granular financial effects from physical and transition risks are not broken out separately. Under full ESRS E1-9 and E1-11 compliance, Danone would need to quantify asset-level exposure, stranded asset risk, and climate opportunity revenue. This remains a gap common across the food & beverage sector.

ESRS 2 GOV · CDP C1
Governance & Accountability
Comprehensive

Danone's climate governance is among the most robust in the consumer goods sector. The Board maintains direct oversight of sustainability and climate strategy. CEO Antoine de Saint-Affrique has climate targets embedded in executive compensation, with the Chief Sustainability Officer operating at the executive committee level. The company's Société à Mission status under French law means sustainability is legally embedded in corporate purpose — a distinction shared by very few companies globally.
