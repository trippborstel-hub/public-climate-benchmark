ESRS E1-1 · CDP C3
Transition Plan for Climate Change Mitigation
Comprehensive

Microsoft committed in January 2020 to be carbon negative by 2030 and by 2050 to remove all carbon the company has emitted since its founding in 1975. This is one of the most ambitious climate commitments in the corporate world — going beyond net zero to a carbon negative posture that includes historical responsibility.

The strategy is underpinned by a $1B Climate Innovation Fund, an internal carbon fee applied to all business groups since 2012 (expanded to include Scope 3 in 2020), and massive renewable energy procurement. However, the explosive growth of AI and cloud infrastructure is creating significant headwinds for Scope 3 reduction.

The AI Paradox

The paradox of Microsoft's climate strategy: industry-leading ambition meets the realities of exponential AI/cloud growth. Scope 1&2 are declining via renewable energy, but Scope 3 from infrastructure expansion may require unprecedented carbon removal investment. Microsoft's transparency about being "not yet on track" for Scope 3 is commendable, but the gap between ambition and trajectory is widening.

ESRS E1-7 · CDP C11
Carbon Credits, Removals & Internal Carbon Fee
Comprehensive

Microsoft is the world's largest corporate carbon removal purchaser — this is its strongest differentiator in climate strategy. The company has contracted approximately 22 million metric tons of carbon removal across multiple durability tiers, spanning short-term neutrality needs, 2030 carbon negative targets, and long-term historic emissions removal through 2050+.

The portfolio includes three durability tiers: Low (forestry, soil carbon), Medium (biochar, CO₂ utilization), and High (direct air capture, BECCS, enhanced mineralization). Partners include Climeworks, Heirloom, and others at the frontier of carbon removal technology.
