ESRS E1-1 · CDP C3
Transition Plan for Climate Change Mitigation
Partial

Abbott's climate strategy is embedded within its 2030 Sustainability Plan, launched in 2020, which includes environmental protection as one of its core pillars. The plan targets a 30% absolute reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2030 from a 2018 baseline, with science-based targets approved by SBTi under a well-below 2°C pathway. However, Abbott has not published a standalone net zero roadmap or committed to a formal net zero target year.

The company's approach centers on energy efficiency improvements in manufacturing, renewable energy procurement, supplier engagement for Scope 3, and sustainable packaging initiatives. Abbott's 2030 Plan operates across four environmental focus areas: energy and GHG emissions, water stewardship, waste management, and packaging.

Analyst Assessment

Abbott's transition plan covers Scope 1 and 2 with clear SBTi-approved targets, but the absence of a formal net zero commitment and the reliance on a supplier-engagement approach for Scope 3 (rather than absolute reduction targets) is a notable gap relative to leading peers. The company has achieved 9% absolute reduction in Scope 1+2 since 2018 — on track for the 30% target by 2030, but behind the pace needed for 1.5°C alignment. The 2030 Plan provides a solid framework, but a standalone climate transition roadmap with quantified investment and milestones would significantly strengthen credibility.

Gap Assessment

Abbott's 30% Scope 1+2 target is a credible near-term commitment, but the well-below 2°C classification and lack of 1.5°C alignment limits the ambition score. The supplier engagement approach for Scope 3 (rather than an absolute reduction target) is the standard SBTi pathway for healthcare companies, but it means Abbott does not have a quantified Scope 3 reduction target. With only ~40% of target suppliers having SBTs by 2024 and a 2026 deadline, this target is at risk. The absence of a net zero commitment is a significant gap relative to peers like J&J (net zero 2045) and Medtronic (net zero 2050).

Abbott's energy strategy focuses on manufacturing efficiency improvements, low-carbon energy investments, and increasing energy awareness across operations. The company has a corporate sustainability fund that has financed 20 energy-related projects since 2023, yielding approximately 3,600 metric tons CO₂e in reductions from the first four completed projects alone.

Unlike many peers, Abbott has not joined RE100 or committed to a 100% renewable electricity target. The company's renewable energy procurement is growing but remains a smaller portion of total energy consumption compared to leading healthcare companies. Energy efficiency in manufacturing remains the primary lever for operational decarbonization.

ESRS E1-2 · E1-3 · CDP C2
Climate Risk Identification & Resilience
Partial

Abbott has conducted climate-related risk assessments consistent with TCFD recommendations, examining physical and transition risks across its global operations. As a diversified healthcare company with manufacturing in 30+ countries, Abbott faces exposure to supply chain disruption, water stress at manufacturing sites, and regulatory risks including carbon pricing and packaging regulations.

The company's water stewardship program is a key resilience measure, with 7 of 11 high-water-impact manufacturing sites in water-stressed areas achieving Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) certification, and an additional six sites implementing internal stewardship practices.

Gap Assessment

Abbott's water stewardship program is a standout resilience measure, with AWS certification providing third-party validation. However, broader climate risk disclosures lack specificity — scenario analysis assumptions, temperature pathways, and quantified financial impacts are not fully disclosed in public reports. Under ESRS E1-2 and E1-3, Abbott would need to provide more detailed scenario modeling and quantified exposure estimates. The Board of Directors and Public Policy Committee provide oversight, but the absence of quantified risk ranges limits investor utility.

ESRS E1-7 · CDP C11
Carbon Credits, Offsets & Removals
Not Disclosed

Abbott does not publicly disclose any use of carbon credits or offsets as part of its climate strategy. The company's SBTi targets are gross absolute reduction targets for Scope 1 and 2 without credit netting, which is consistent with SBTi requirements. There are no known carbon-neutral product claims or voluntary offset programs disclosed in Abbott's sustainability reports.

Abbott's approach focuses entirely on operational emissions reductions through efficiency and energy procurement, rather than offsetting or removal strategies. No internal carbon removal programs or nature-based solution investments have been publicly announced.

Assessment

The absence of carbon credit reliance is positive from a credibility standpoint — Abbott's targets are based purely on operational and supply chain reductions. However, the lack of a net zero target also means there is no articulated strategy for addressing residual emissions. If Abbott were to set a net zero commitment in the future, a removals strategy would be required for the final 5–10% of hard-to-abate emissions, particularly in manufacturing and logistics.

CSRD Readiness Gap

ESRS E1-9 and E1-11 require quantified financial effects from physical and transition risks, reconciled to financial statements. Abbott currently provides only qualitative risk disclosures in its sustainability reports, with climate risks appearing generically in SEC filings. The absence of climate-specific CapEx disclosure, quantified risk exposure, and internal carbon pricing represents one of the most significant ESRS readiness gaps. This is common among US-domiciled healthcare companies, but Abbott's EU operations and revenue exposure will likely require improved granularity under CSRD.

All corporate officers carry a human capital goal tied to executive compensation.
