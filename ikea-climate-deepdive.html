ESRS E1-1 · CDP C3
Transition Plan for Climate Change Mitigation
Comprehensive

IKEA has published one of the most ambitious climate transition plans in global retail. The People & Planet Positive strategy — now in its third iteration — commits the IKEA value chain to become "climate positive" by 2030, meaning IKEA aims to reduce more greenhouse gas emissions than its entire value chain emits. In June 2024, the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) formally validated IKEA's net-zero targets, confirming alignment with a 1.5°C pathway.

The transition plan spans all Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions across the full IKEA franchise system — from raw material extraction (52% of footprint) through product use at home (17%) and end-of-life (8%). IKEA explicitly rejects reliance on carbon offsets, committing to gross absolute emissions reductions across the value chain. Ingka Group, which operates ~90% of IKEA stores, has invested EUR 4.3B in renewable energy and committed EUR 7.5B by 2030.

Analyst Assessment

IKEA stands out as one of the most ambitious companies on climate globally. The combination of SBTi-validated 1.5°C targets, explicit rejection of offsets, massive renewable energy investment (EUR 7.5B), and a 28% absolute reduction already achieved by FY24 makes IKEA's transition plan among the most credible in retail. The key execution challenge remains materials (52% of footprint) — decarbonizing a global supply chain of ~1,600 suppliers across 50+ countries. The reframing from "Climate Positive 2030" to "Net Zero and Beyond" signals pragmatic ambition.

IKEA is one of the largest private investors in renewable energy globally. Ingka Group — operating ~90% of IKEA stores — has invested EUR 4.3 billion in wind and solar energy assets and committed a total of EUR 7.5 billion by 2030 to produce 15 terawatt-hours of renewable energy annually. The company operates 47 wind farms and 26 solar parks, with a combined installed capacity exceeding 1.7 GW.

Ingka Group reached 100% renewable electricity for its own operations by FY20 and maintains 96.6% renewable electricity as of FY24. Remaining gaps are in markets with limited grid access to renewables. Beyond electricity, IKEA is addressing thermal energy through heat pumps, biogas, and electrification of heating systems in stores and warehouses.

Climate Risk Identification & Resilience
Partial

IKEA has conducted climate scenario analysis aligned with TCFD recommendations, informing its net-zero goals validated by SBTi. As the world's largest furniture retailer — dependent on wood, cotton, plastics, metals, and complex global supply chains — IKEA faces significant exposure to both physical climate risks and transition risks. The company's business consequence analysis supports the FY30 roadmap, though detailed quantified financial impacts remain limited in public disclosures.

Gap Assessment

IKEA's climate risk identification is directionally strong — the company recognizes its concentrated exposure in materials (52% of footprint) and has clear adaptation strategies through sustainable sourcing, circularity, and renewable energy. However, quantified financial impact estimates for specific risks are not fully disclosed in public reports. Under ESRS E1-2 and E1-3, IKEA would need to provide more granular scenario assumptions, financial quantification, and asset-level resilience assessments. The dual reporting structure (Inter IKEA + Ingka) also complicates consolidated risk reporting.

ESRS E1-7 · CDP C11
Carbon Credits, Offsets & Removals
Comprehensive

IKEA takes one of the most principled positions on carbon offsets among major corporations: the company explicitly rejects reliance on offsets to meet its climate targets. All IKEA reduction targets are gross absolute — no netting of credits against emissions. The FY50 net-zero commitment requires at least 90% absolute reduction, with only residual emissions neutralized through carbon removal and storage within the IKEA value chain — not through external offset purchases.

This position reflects IKEA's view that offsets do not substitute for real emissions reductions and that the company should invest in decarbonizing its own value chain rather than paying to offset elsewhere. For residual emissions, IKEA is exploring in-chain carbon removal through forestry management, soil carbon sequestration, and bio-based materials that store carbon in products.

Standout Position

IKEA's explicit rejection of offsets — combined with SBTi-validated gross absolute targets — places the company in a small minority of large corporations that refuse to use carbon credits as part of their climate strategy. This is noteworthy given IKEA's scale (~225,000 co-workers, 60+ markets) and the fact that many peers in retail and consumer goods rely heavily on offsets and carbon-neutral claims. The in-chain removal approach (forestry, soil carbon) is credible given IKEA's position as one of the world's largest buyers of wood.

Anticipated Financial Effects
Partial

As a private company, IKEA's financial disclosures are more limited than publicly listed peers. However, IKEA has demonstrated exceptional financial commitment to climate action — particularly through Ingka Group's EUR 7.5B renewable energy investment program, making it one of the largest private clean-energy investors globally. IKEA frames climate action as a business enabler rather than a cost center, noting that revenue has grown 24% since FY16 while emissions have fallen 28%.

CSRD Readiness Gap

ESRS E1-9 and E1-11 require quantified financial effects from physical and transition risks, reconciled to financial statements. IKEA currently provides directional investment figures (EUR 7.5B in RE) and qualitative opportunity descriptions, but not the granular risk quantification, asset-level exposure analysis, or financial statement reconciliation that CSRD demands. The private ownership structure means IKEA currently has no legal obligation for these disclosures — but as a franchisor operating across Europe, CSRD alignment will become increasingly relevant for stakeholder expectations.

Governance & Accountability
Comprehensive

IKEA's climate governance is structured across the franchise system through the Strategic Sustainability Council, chaired by the CEO of Inter IKEA Group. This council includes managing directors from Inter IKEA Group and franchisees (including Ingka Group), ensuring alignment on climate targets across the ~90% of IKEA stores operated by Ingka and the remaining franchise network. A dedicated Chief Sustainability Officer leads Inter IKEA Group's sustainability management team, with sustainability managers embedded across three core businesses: Retail Concept, Range & Supply.

IKEA has integrated sustainability into its core business strategy through the People & Planet Positive framework, with objectives and key results (OKRs) extending through 2030. The company reports annually through both its Sustainability Report and a dedicated Climate Report, demonstrating sector-leading transparency for a private company.

Governance Strength

IKEA's governance model is notable for its cross-franchise coordination — aligning climate targets across a complex system of independent franchisees through the Strategic Sustainability Council. The CEO-chaired council provides the highest level of oversight. The dual Climate Report + Sustainability Report publishing cadence exceeds most private companies' disclosure levels. The main governance gap is the lack of disclosed executive compensation linkage to climate targets — though this is common among private companies.

Data Compilation Notes

This climate strategy profile was compiled from publicly available sources including the IKEA Climate Report FY24, Inter IKEA Group Sustainability Report FY24, Ingka Group Annual Summary & Sustainability Report (FY24 and FY25), CDP Climate Change questionnaire responses, SBTi Target Dashboard, and IKEA's public climate data pages. "IKEA retail & other operations" (~2% of total) roughly corresponds to Scope 1+2, while remaining categories map to Scope 3. Revenue figures reference IKEA retail sales (all franchisees) unless noted. Inter IKEA Group revenue (wholesale + franchise fees) was EUR 26.5B in FY24.
